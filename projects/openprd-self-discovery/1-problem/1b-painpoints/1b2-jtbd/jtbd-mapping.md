## Cross-Job Dependencies

```
J1 (Get Started) ──→ J3 (Quant-Qual Synthesis) ──→ J2 (Deliver Outputs)
         │                      │                         ↑
         │                      └───→ J4 (Growth) ───────┘
         │                                                ↑
         └──────────────────────→ J5 (Team Scale) ───────┘
```

- **J1 is prerequisite for all**: If users can't get started, no other job matters
- **J3 feeds J2 and J4**: Better synthesis = better outputs and better experiment design
- **J5 amplifies all jobs**: Collaboration multiplies the value of every other job
- **J2 is the delivery endpoint**: All jobs ultimately serve the goal of delivering actionable outputs

## Strategic Implications

1. **J2 (Output Delivery) and J3 (Quant-Qual) have the highest opportunity scores.** These represent the biggest gaps between importance and satisfaction. Fixing these would convert "sometimes tool" users into daily users. [Source: painpoint-mapping.md]

2. **J1 (Get Started) is a binary gate.** Unlike other jobs where poor performance reduces value, poor onboarding eliminates the entire user relationship. The 4-source convergence on CLI as barrier (C6) makes this the highest-priority structural fix. [Source: cross-source-differentiation.md]

3. **J4 (Growth Discovery) is a high-value niche.** Tom's segment has the clearest unmet workflow — "nobody else is serving" this. Could be a differentiation wedge even if other jobs are partially addressed by competitors. [Source: tom-analysis.md]

4. **J5 (Team Scale) is the enterprise unlock.** PP-28 (SOC 2/SSO) and PP-25 (collaboration) are binary blockers for the highest-WTP segment ($500-1000/seat/month). [Source: aisha-analysis.md]
